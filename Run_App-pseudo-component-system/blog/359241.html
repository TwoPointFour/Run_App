The progress I started making changed the way I approached running for the rest of my high school career. Although there were many other periods where I would go through a period of stagnation, each time that happened, there were some specific things that could be attributed to it. And when I discovered certain things that helped me break out of these periods – my mindset and approach to running would change again for the better.
Let me start off by saying that I didn't know how to train for running in high school. I started out my freshman and sophomore years with very little knowledge of what it took to be good at something like track or cross country. By the time I had gained any real level of success at the varsity level, I noticed something: most people who do have passion for running only ever compete on a high-school level. From this realization, I've learned that if you want to succeed beyond high school, then there are some important factors you should be aware of upon entering your first training regimen.
I remember my freshman and sophomore years with very little knowledge of what it took to be good at something like track or cross country. By the time I had gained any real level of success at the varsity level, my coaches started to stress about repetition we could have been doing that would have made me faster.
I shook at the thought of a college coach ever looking at my running times. The abstract concept of “repetition” didn't matter to me. Even if it would have led to future success, I was more concerned with enjoying life the way I wanted to rather than worrying about doing things the “right way”. Ultimately, I made a lot of mistakes in my approach to running college cross country and track. To this day it bugs me that a simple lack of repetition almost prevented me from becoming a faster runner and getting recruited by a Division I school.

In 2011, I won a fourth marathon. It took 14 years of training and racing to reach that point – 14 years that were full of ups and downs, progress and setbacks, joys and frustrations.
